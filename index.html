From 'Palm' to Prison: How Tiny Corrections Escalate Relationship Conflict

A Reflective Essay on Mansplaining, Escalation, and the Silent Letter That Destroyed an Evening
Author: Somil · Audience: One · Stakes: High

On that call, I made a mistake.

Not a big one. Not the kind that ends things. A small, avoidable, completely unnecessary one: I corrected how Viki said "palm."

The L is silent, I explained. With confidence. With detail.

Viki did not ask me to explain this.

Viki was just talking. And I interrupted to deliver a phonetics lesson she did not request, did not need, and did not appreciate.

That is mansplaining. Not the loud, dramatic kind. The quiet, well-meaning kind. The kind where you genuinely think you are helping. And that makes it worse.

· · ·

Viki expressed disagreement. I elaborated. Viki expressed irritation. I clarified. Viki hung up.

I sat there, technically correct, completely alone.

The moment I should have stopped was the first moment. Every sentence after that was just damage.

Here is what I have learned: being right about a word is not the same as being right about what matters. Viki did not need a dictionary. Viki needed me to listen.

Going forward, if I feel the urge to correct something, I am going to stop, nod my head, and say yes.

I am sorry. Not for knowing about the L. For thinking that was something worth saying.

You matter more than a silent letter. You always will.

· · ·
Submitted with genuine remorse and moderate embarrassment,
Somil 🌴
(the 'L' in his name is, for what it's worth, fully pronounced)

Appendix A: Lessons Learned

  • Unsolicited corrections are a form of conversational littering.
  • The phrase "actually, let me explain" should be treated as a red flag in one's own mouth.
  • Phonetic accuracy and emotional safety are not competing values. One of them is a value; the other is a pub quiz answer.
  • When she hangs up, the debate is over. You lost. The letter lost. Everyone lost.
  • Tea. Always offer tea.
